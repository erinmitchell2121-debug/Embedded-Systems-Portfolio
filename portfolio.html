Power Optimization of a Continuous Glucose Monitoring (CGM) System

August 2024-May 2025

Problem:

CGM battery life only lasted a few hours. This is not sufficient in serving the needs of those who must monitor their glucose levels at various points throughout the day without charging it every few hours.

Solution:

Power the implantable glucose sensor on a lower duty cycle in order to reduce power consumption and extend the life of the externally worn battery. However, the rate at which the subcutaneous electrochemical sensor is switched on and off at could buildup interstitial ions around the implant and affect the signal output of the sensor if there isnt a proper sensor stabilization period. To meet specifications signal output had to be within a 5% margin of error.

Contributions:

      Formulated pulse width modulation (pwm) algorithms in C++ to operate the systems microcontroller at various duties cycles.

      Formulated an algorithm to extract necessary parameters to process sensor output. Created MATLAB script to continuously graph processed output signal.

      All algorithms were written in Arduino IDE and Segger Embedded Studios to compile on an UNO R3 and a BMD-340 for initial and final iterations, respectively.

      A breakout PCB of the implantable miniaturized sensor components was designed (Eagle Autodesk Fusion) and interfaced with the respective microcontrollers to test pwm algorithms on sensor output.

      For each duty cycle variation, sensor output was collected using the same glucose concentration and across the same duration of time. Different modes of percent error were used to determine the percent error of sensor output for each duty cycle variation.

Outcome:

While all duty cycle variations were under 5% margin of error, as expected lower duty cycles led to higher error most likely related to the insufficient time for the sensor to stabilize. However, even the higher duty cycles implemented by the pwm algorithms were able to extend battery life by around 60%.
